Explanation of various quantities used in {{ config.WSNAME }}:


Definition of different quantities:

Completion (%)
This number presents the percentage of full-time AND first-time students who complete at four-year/less-than-four-year institutions within 150% of expected time to completion (e.g., within 6 years for a four-year school).
In the ed.gov dataset, the relevant metrics are C150_4 and C150_L4.

Debt/median debt (USD)
We look at the median original loan amount as students enter the repayment program.
In the ed.gov dataset, this quantity is called DEBT_MDN.

Earning/mean earning (USD)
As a proxy to understand earning after graduation, we look at the mean earnings of students who are working and not enrolled 6 years after their entry to the school.
In the ed.gov dataset, the relevant metric is MN_EARN_WNE_P6.

Expenses per student USD
In this quantity we look at the reported value of how much schools spend on instructional expenditures per full-time equivalent student (per year?).
In the ed.gov dataset, the relevant metric is INEXPFTE.

We divide the official number by the median income in the school's county, to adjust for the fact that similar instructional effort may vary substantially from one part of the country to another.

Full-time/FT faculty (%)
This quantity tracks the proportion of faculty that is full-time.
In the ed.gov dataset, the relevant metric is PFTFAC.

Full-time/FT student (%)
This number represents the percentage of entering undergraduates who are first-time and full-time students.
In the ed.gov dataset, the relevant metric is PFTFTUG1_EF.

Net price (USD)
This is the average net price for Title IV public, private for-profit and nonprofit institutions. This takes into account the "sticker price" (see below), and subtracts the average grant/scholarship aid.
In the ed.gov dataset, the relevant metrics are NPT4_PUB and NPT4_PRIV.

Pell recipient (%)
This is the percentage of undergraduates who receive a Pell Grant.
In the ed.gov dataset, this quantity is called PCTPELL.

Student retention(%)
Student retention percentage is a sum of the retention percentage of the part-time students and the retention percentage of the full-time students. We arrive at each of these quantities by multiplying the relevant retention percentage for the relevant student group (part-time or full-time) with their fraction of the total undergraduate student population.

For the four-year institutions, these quantities are referred to as PFTFTUG1_EF, RET_PT4, and RET_FT4 in the ed.gov dataset. For less-than-four-year schools, the last 2 quantities are RET_PTL4, and RET_FTL4.

Sticker price/Avg. cost (USD)
The average annual total cost of attendance, including tuition and fees, books and supplies, and living expenses for all full-time, first-time, degree/certificate-seeking undergraduates who receive Title IV aid. It is taken directly from the ed.gov dataset. In that dataset, the related quantities are CostT4_A for academic-year institutions.

Withdrwal (%)
Percent withdrawn from original institution within 6 years. In the ed.gov dataset, this is WDRAW_ORIG_YR6_RT.


value score (out of 100)
To define value score, we first calculate a value_index, defined as a product of 3 ratios: (a) earning to debt ratio of graduates, (b) % completion to % admission rate, and (c) sticker price to net price. Once we have the value index of all the schools, we calculate percentile based on similar schools (based on their Carnegie classification CCBASIC in ed.gov dataset).

Care score (out of 100)
Similar to value score defined above, calculating care score involves first calculating a care index. Care index is defined as the product of % Pell grant recipients, % of graduates, % of students who don't withdraw, retention %, % of full-time faculty, and the ratio of expenses on students squared to average faculty salary. Once a care index is calculated, the percentile score calculated based on the school's Carnegies classification is defined as the care score.

Data source, analysis and visualization

Where do all these numbers come from?
We are mostly using data available from collegescorecard.ed.gov. For comparing $ amounts where applicable, we use county-level income data available from datausa.io.

Does {{ config.WSNAME }} directly report the numbers from the data soure?

We report most of the quantities mentioned in our website directly from ed.gov website. There are a few exceptions, however. For example, "value score" and "care score" are combined metrics that we created to better compare performances among schools.

Also, we divided the average faculty salary and average expenses on full-time students by a factor that scales with the median family income in the school's location.

In addition, the Wikipedia and the social media data come directly from the respective sources.
